The ten-thousand-hour trade.

What Malcolm Gladwell got right, what he got wrong, and why the most valuable asset a trader can accumulate isn't capital — it's a taxonomy of their own mistakes. A field manual for building craft in public.

By David Justin 22 · September · 2025 A long read · 18 min

In 2008 Malcolm Gladwell popularised an idea from the psychologist Anders Ericsson: that world-class expertise in almost any domain requires roughly ten thousand hours of deliberate practice. The number was an average, not a threshold, and Ericsson spent the rest of his life gently correcting the record. By then the number had escaped him. It still has not come back.

Nowhere is the ten-thousand-hour myth more corrosive than in markets. I have watched otherwise thoughtful people log ten thousand hours of screen time and mistake it for expertise. They are not the same thing. Ericsson's whole contribution — the part Gladwell compressed out — was that the hours only count if they are deliberate. Most hours are not.

What deliberate means

Ericsson's definition had four pieces. You need a task just outside your current ability. Immediate feedback. Full concentration. And opportunity for repetition with correction. Any hour missing any of these four ingredients doesn't count toward expertise. Ten thousand such hours grow you. Twenty thousand of the wrong kind can actively harm you, because they ossify bad habits into instincts.

A trader sitting at a desk for ten hours watching price action is not practising. They are performing. The difference is enormous and almost nobody talks about it.

You don't get better by trading more. You get better by trading the same way on purpose, losing in a structured way, and writing down exactly what you misjudged before the market opens again.

The taxonomy

The most useful habit I've ever built — more useful than any chart pattern, any indicator, any newsletter — is keeping what I call the mistake taxonomy. It is a single text file. Every losing trade goes in. Every mistake gets a category. Over time, categories merge, split, sharpen. After three years mine has stabilised into seven.

Mistake 01 · Size before conviction.

Taking a position larger than the quality of your thesis justifies. This one dominates every new trader's first 500 hours. It feels like confidence. It is actually impatience dressed in a suit.

Mistake 02 · Right idea, wrong instrument.

Being correct on the macro view but expressing it through a vehicle whose mechanics work against you — leverage decay, funding rates, illiquid spreads. The market rewards correct directional calls only when the plumbing doesn't eat them first.

Mistake 03 · Conviction inflation.

Mistaking the loudness of your belief for the strength of your evidence. I keep a line in my journal: how much new information have I seen today vs. how much have I just repeated to myself? Repetition is not evidence. It is a feeling.

Mistake 04 · Anchoring to entry.

Managing a position from where you got in instead of from where the market now is. Entry price is a historical artefact; it does not care about you, and neither should you care about it after the trade is on.

Mistake 05 · The comeback trade.

Taking a second position specifically to recover the loss from the first. This is the single fastest way to turn a bad day into a bad year.

Mistake 06 · Process drift under profit.

Subtly changing your rules after a run of wins because the rules feel unnecessarily conservative. They are not. The rules are what produced the wins. Loosening them is how you give back in one week what you earned in six.

Mistake 07 · Trading your mood.

Opening or closing positions based on your emotional state rather than the market's state. I solved this with a small, stupid, reliable rule: if I cannot write a single paragraph describing why this trade is on, the trade comes off.

Seven mistakes. Every loss in fifteen years fits inside one of them. The categories are the real asset.

Why the taxonomy compounds

A bad trade that you cannot categorise is a bad trade twice. First you lose the money. Then you lose the lesson. A bad trade that fits neatly into a pre-existing category is, paradoxically, a profitable event: the loss is the tuition, the category is the product, and the product is the thing that keeps you in the game long enough for the hours to become deliberate.

This is what Ericsson meant by feedback. Not the PnL — everyone gets the PnL. The feedback is the diagnosis. And the only person qualified to diagnose your trades is you, because you are the only person who knows what you were thinking when you clicked.

A protocol

If you want to run this experiment on yourself, here is the protocol I teach at the academy. It takes about twenty minutes a day and is the single most important thing I've ever shared with a cohort.

  1. Before market open — write one paragraph describing the single trade you are most likely to take today, and the single reason you might be wrong about it. Save it.
  2. After every closed position — paste the entry, exit, and PnL at the top of a new entry. Underneath, answer three questions: what did I actually think would happen? what actually happened? what category does the delta belong to?
  3. After market close — re-read the morning paragraph. Highlight every sentence that turned out to be right. Highlight in a different colour every sentence that was wrong. Do not analyse. Just mark.
  4. On Sundays — read the week. Look for the sentence that appears, almost word-for-word, across multiple days. That sentence is either a conviction worth pressing or a delusion worth killing. It is never neutral.

Do this for twelve weeks. Do not add a single new strategy, indicator, or instrument during those weeks. At the end of it you will have, in your own handwriting, the seven categories you actually lose money in. They will not be my seven. They will be yours, which is the whole point.

The part Gladwell got right

It is fashionable now to dunk on the ten-thousand-hour idea. I want to defend it, partly. Gladwell was wrong about the number, but he was right about the shape: mastery is time-denominated. It cannot be shortcut. It cannot be bought. It cannot be inherited. It takes years, in the specific sense that you cannot compress three years of market cycles into three months of simulation, no matter how good the simulator.

This is an unfashionable thing to say in an industry that sells courses, signals, and six-month transformations. I run a school, and I will tell you the same thing I tell every cohort on day one: we can give you a vocabulary, a framework, a community, and a taxonomy. We cannot give you the years. Only the years give you the years.

The ten-thousand-hour trade is not a trade. It's the decision to sit at the desk long enough for the desk to teach you what the books cannot. Most people won't. The ones who do aren't smarter. They're just patient enough to let their own mistakes compound into a curriculum.

That's the whole game. Thanks for reading.
