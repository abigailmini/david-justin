Essay №002 Speaking · Education 12 min read

897 chairs and the weight of three days.

The night before the record attempt at MITEC, I walked into an empty hall and counted the chairs. Here's what I learned about presence, preparation, and why the hardest part of a three-day Guinness World Record wasn't the record at all.

By David Justin 02 · October · 2025 MITEC Kuala Lumpur · Retrospective

At 11:47 PM on the night of 12 November 2022, I let myself back into the empty main hall at MITEC — Malaysia International Trade and Exhibition Centre — with a cup of tea and the kind of anxiety that has no verb attached to it. The Guinness adjudicator was arriving at 7:00 AM. Registration opened at 7:30. Day one of the three-day record attempt began at 9:00. My team had gone home. The venue lights were on a timer and the timer had another eight minutes.

I counted the chairs. There were nine hundred exactly. We needed 800 registered learners in them — and the same learners back again on day two and day three — for the attempt to qualify. I counted the chairs again because the number felt abstract and I wanted it to feel real.

The problem with big numbers

Big numbers are easy to say and almost impossible to feel. "Nearly a thousand people" is a phrase. Nine hundred chairs is a room. There is no useful way to prepare for the first one. There is a very specific way to prepare for the second.

I had given talks to fifty people before. Two hundred once. Four hundred, on a mixed-media stage at a conference, where half the audience was watching on overflow screens and therefore not really there. A fully-occupied room of nine hundred people who had all specifically shown up to listen to me explain blockchain was not a problem I had ever prepared for, because I had never faced it.

The record wasn't three days of teaching. The record was three days of not losing the room. Those are different skills and I didn't fully understand that until the chairs filled up on day one.

Three rehearsals

The team and I had built the curriculum over six months. Three complete run-throughs of the day-one opener, because day one is the day the adjudicator decides whether the attempt is live. The first rehearsal was a disaster; I ran 18 minutes long and my partner — who had sat through every version of every lesson I'd ever given — told me, with genuine kindness, that I sounded like I was delivering a TED talk about blockchain instead of teaching blockchain. She was right.

The second rehearsal I threw away the polish. I taught it like I would teach a dinner party. I came in 47 seconds under time. It was the warmest the lesson had ever felt, and the least structured. My team gently told me it would be impossible to maintain that energy in a room of 900 strangers.

The third rehearsal split the difference and I hated it. The morning of the attempt, I opened my notes one last time, deleted seven slides, and decided I'd rather fail warm than succeed cold.

What actually happened

At 8:47 AM on day one the hall was full. The Guinness adjudicator — a British woman named Beatrice with a clipboard and the unhurried calm of someone who has seen a thousand people try to set a thousand records — was in the front row, her stopwatch already in her hand.

I walked out. I did not say hello. I held up my phone, showed the room a MetaMask transaction sending 0.01 ETH, and asked the audience to count along with me to twelve as the transaction confirmed. Twelve seconds. Nine hundred voices. It was the most terrifying and beautiful thing I have ever been part of — and we still had two more days in front of us.

Nine hundred voices counting to twelve. That was the moment I knew the three days would hold.

What I learned

One · The chairs taught me more than the slides.

Walking into that empty room the night before, counting to nine hundred, turned an abstract number into a specific room. Every speaker I've mentored since, I've told the same thing: go see the room empty first. A room empty is honest. A room full is overwhelming. The geometry you learn at midnight carries you through the morning.

Two · The hardest hour is the one before.

I was more nervous between 8:30 and 9:00 on day one than I was at any point across the three days that followed. Once the lesson started, I had a job. Before it started, I had an imagination. The imagination was the enemy. Every public speaker I respect has some version of this. None of them have solved it. We've all just made peace with the fact that the green room is the hardest room in the building.

Three · Record-breaking is a team sport.

Guinness certifies the individual, which is a polite fiction. The record was won by the 14 people who did the work I didn't see: the two who called every registered participant the week before to confirm, the one who built the QR system, the photographer who ran the stage-left camera for all three days without a single cutaway to their own feet, the operations lead who handled the adjudicator's paperwork while I was warming up. If they'd been named on the certificate it would have read like the credits to a film.

Why I'm still writing about this

It's been more than two years since the record. I haven't written about it in detail until now because I wanted to be sure of what I'd learned before I said it out loud. The thing I finally want to say is that the record was never the point. The record was the forcing function.

I wanted to build an education business that took blockchain seriously as a subject — not as a marketing lane, not as a token-launch pipeline. I suspected, correctly, that very few people would take us seriously unless we had something undeniable to point at. The record is that thing. Nobody argues with a Guinness certificate. Nobody argues with 897 people filling a hall at MITEC for three days straight.

What the record actually bought us was the right to be boring for the next five years. The right to teach slowly, thoroughly, in two languages, to small cohorts, without anyone demanding another stunt. The right to build curriculum instead of spectacle.

Every time I walk past a lecture hall now, I count the chairs. I can't help it. Nine hundred was a ceiling. One at a time is the whole job.
