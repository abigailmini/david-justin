Essay №002 Speaking · Education 10 min read

897 chairs and the weight of three days.

Numbers look clean in headlines. In real life they become chairs, aisles, witness forms, water bottles, timing sheets, and the pressure of keeping a room present for three straight days.

By David Justin 02 · October · 2025 MITEC Kuala Lumpur

The number that lived in the press coverage was 897. Clean, memorable, official. But long before it became a number, it was a room. It was rows of chairs at MITEC Kuala Lumpur, measured distances between sections, registration flow, attendance checks, and the silent question every organiser asks before the doors open: will the energy hold once real people sit in the seats?

The night before the Guinness World Record attempt, I walked the hall after almost everyone had gone home. The stage lights were dimmed. Tape still marked some of the floor. A few last-minute changes were happening near the back. I remember looking across the room and realising that large events do something strange to your brain. They make scale feel theatrical until you stand in the middle of it alone. Then scale becomes physical.

The chairs made the challenge honest

People say "nearly a thousand attendees" very easily. It sounds impressive and distant. But when you stand in front of 897 verified seats, the abstraction disappears. You start thinking in practical questions. Can people see clearly from the side sections? How long will roll call take? What happens if the energy dips on the second afternoon? Which transition between modules is most likely to drag? Chairs have a way of forcing honesty. They turn ambition into logistics.

That was useful for me because public speaking can sometimes tempt people into performance mode. You begin to think about momentum, applause, and memorable lines. A record attempt punishes that instinct. Guinness does not care about your aura. It cares about criteria, verification, continuity, and whether what happened can withstand scrutiny afterward. The chairs reminded me that this was not a show pretending to be education. It had to be real education strong enough to survive the paperwork.

A world record is glamorous only from the outside. From the inside it feels like disciplined repetition under a brighter light.

Three days is a different kind of burden

A one-hour keynote asks for concentration. Three days ask for stewardship. You are not merely opening strong. You are managing trust over time. Day one is excitement. Day two is the test. Day three is where fatigue becomes visible unless the material has genuine shape and the room feels carried rather than dragged.

That was the real challenge behind the 897 chairs. It wasn't simply about filling them. It was about earning the return. Learners had to keep showing up. Witnesses had to verify attendance properly. The lesson had to remain coherent and engaging enough that the structure held together. The logistics team and I spent more time thinking about pacing than spectacle. Where do people need a reset? When should the examples become simpler, not denser? How do you help a large room feel taught instead of processed?

I learned that scale magnifies small weaknesses. A slightly unclear explanation becomes a real drag in a hall that size. A transition that feels acceptable in rehearsal becomes expensive when multiplied across hundreds of attention spans. Preparation became less about sounding impressive and more about removing friction.

That lesson stayed with me long after the certificate arrived. Good public education is not built from dramatic moments alone. It is built from clear sequencing, repeated emphasis, and enough humility to notice when the room needs you to slow down. The larger the room, the more disciplined that humility has to become.

Why 897 still matters to me

People sometimes ask why I mention the exact number instead of rounding it up for effect. The answer is simple. Precision honors the reality of what happened. "About a thousand" is headline language. "897" is human language. It reflects the actual verified result, the actual learners, the actual weight carried by the team, and the actual standard met that day. Precision keeps memory from becoming mythology.

It also reminds me that education is built from countable things. One learner understanding custody for the first time. One section of the room leaning forward because an example finally clicked. One break where someone tells you they had never thought about blockchain as infrastructure instead of speculation. Big outcomes are almost always collections of small, countable shifts.

"897" matters because it keeps the story factual, and facts are what let trust compound.

The team behind the number

No honest version of this story makes the record look individual. My name may be attached to it, but the number belongs to a system of people doing their part without drama. Registration staff keeping lines moving. Operations people handling verification. Venue crew solving problems before they became visible. Media teams documenting what happened without disrupting it. Witnesses paying attention to details most audiences never notice. Those roles are not glamorous, but they are exactly what made the result credible.

I came away with more respect for organisers than ever. Good education events look smooth because somebody cared about every unglamorous hinge. The chairs were there because somebody placed them. The pathways were clear because somebody measured them. The day stayed calm because somebody anticipated what could go wrong and quietly reduced the odds.

I think audiences feel this more than speakers realize. People can tell when a room has been respected. They can feel when an event was designed to teach rather than merely impress. That feeling creates trust, and trust is usually the difference between a memorable event and a meaningful one.

What the room taught me after the applause

The record became useful later, once the headlines moved on. It gave us a reference point, yes, but more importantly it changed my standard for what preparation means. I became less interested in impressive launches and more interested in durable teaching. If you can hold a room that size for three days, you stop romanticising noise. You start valuing clarity, pacing, and trust much more than hype.

Even now, whenever I enter a venue, I instinctively count chairs. Not because I want another record, but because chairs are still the clearest way I know to remember the responsibility of teaching. Every seat is a person who gave you time. Every row is a promise to be prepared. That is what the 897 chairs really left behind. Not just a record, but a discipline.
