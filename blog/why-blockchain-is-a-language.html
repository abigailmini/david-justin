Why blockchain is a language, not a product.

The most useful way to teach blockchain is not as a shiny tool people buy into, but as a language people gradually learn to read, write, and think with.

By David Justin 14 · October · 2025 Kuala Lumpur

Most people are introduced to blockchain through products. An exchange app. A token launch. A wallet prompt. A chart. From that point onward, they keep asking product questions. Is this coin good? Which chain is fastest? Which app is winning? Those are not useless questions, but they are downstream of a more important one: what is blockchain actually for people to learn?

My answer is simple. Blockchain is a language. Not in a poetic sense, but in a practical one. It has vocabulary, rules, syntax, communities of use, and consequences for fluency. Once you see it that way, a lot of confusion disappears. So does a lot of hype.

A product is something you buy. A language is something you inhabit

The product frame encourages passivity. It makes people ask whether blockchain will be delivered to them, packaged for them, or simplified until no learning is required. But every meaningful technological shift creates a new layer of literacy. The internet did. Mobile did. Social media did. Blockchain does too. You do not truly benefit from a new literacy by standing outside it and waiting for good marketing. You benefit by becoming more fluent inside it.

Think about what fluency looks like in any language. First you recognise symbols. Then you start to understand recurring patterns. Eventually you stop translating word by word and begin to think more naturally in that system. Something similar happens with blockchain. At first a wallet address looks alien. A smart contract feels intimidating. Gas fees seem arbitrary. But over time the system develops internal logic. Transfers, custody, consensus, and ownership stop feeling abstract and start feeling coherent.

The breakthrough is not when someone buys their first token. The breakthrough is when they finally understand what the system is saying back to them.

Why this matters for education

If blockchain is a language, then most education in the space has been badly sequenced. We begin with jargon, because insiders mistake terminology for understanding. We explain consensus algorithms before learners understand why distributed agreement matters. We discuss tokenomics before learners understand digital ownership. It is like teaching grammar tables before letting anyone hear a sentence spoken aloud.

In practice, better teaching starts with lived examples. What does it mean to hold something directly? Why does an open ledger change trust? What happens when money moves with internet-native speed? Learners do not need less rigor. They need the rigor arranged in the right order. The sequence matters because confidence is built through comprehension, not intimidation.

This is especially important in Malaysia, where crypto curiosity is high but literacy is uneven. People are exposed to headlines, influencers, and speculative narratives long before they are given a durable mental model. That makes them vulnerable to cycles of excitement and disappointment. A language-based frame is steadier. It says: before you speculate, learn the alphabet. Before you debate the future, learn the grammar of the present.

I think this is why so many beginners feel either overexcited or embarrassed around crypto. They sense there is a real system underneath the noise, but they have only been handed fragments. A language framework gives them permission to be a beginner properly. You are not supposed to be fluent on day one. You are supposed to notice patterns, make mistakes, and grow more precise over time.

What fluency actually looks like

Fluency is not memorising definitions. It is being able to navigate unfamiliar situations without immediately panicking. A fluent blockchain user can inspect a transaction, understand what a wallet is authorising, recognize when custody is being delegated, and ask sensible questions when something feels off. Fluency gives people judgment, not just vocabulary.

The same is true for builders and investors. A builder with real fluency can tell whether a product genuinely uses the properties of public blockchains or is merely borrowing the branding. An investor with fluency can distinguish infrastructure from narrative. An educator with fluency can sense when a learner is lost, not because they failed to memorize terms, but because the underlying frame has not clicked yet.

Literacy lets you repeat the words. Fluency lets you make sense inside the system.

Why the language frame is also more honest

One reason I prefer this frame is that it is harder to weaponise. Product framing attracts exaggerated promises. Every new chain claims to solve everything. Every new app claims to unlock adoption. Language framing is humbler. Languages do not solve life. They expand the range of possible expression. They let people coordinate differently. They open doors, but they do not automatically walk anyone through them.

That makes for less dramatic marketing, but much better expectations. Blockchain is not one product competing against another. It is a growing literacy layer sitting beneath products, markets, and institutions. Some businesses will win on top of it. Some will disappear. The literacy itself is the more durable story.

Once you accept that, the right question for schools, communities, and even companies becomes obvious. Not "how do we sell this faster?" but "how do we help more people read what is already here?" That shift sounds subtle, but it changes the tone of everything. It replaces urgency with formation, and hype with competence.

What this changes for the next generation

If we teach blockchain as a language, then the mission shifts. We are no longer trying to recruit believers. We are trying to develop readers. Readers of wallets, readers of networks, readers of incentives, readers of digital ownership. That is a healthier ambition and, frankly, a more sustainable one.

The people who will benefit most from this technology over the next decade are unlikely to be the loudest speculators. They will be the ones who can move calmly between systems, understand what they are signing, and explain the logic to others in plain language. In other words, the people who become bilingual in the old financial world and the new on-chain one.

That's why I keep teaching it this way. Once learners stop asking, "Which product should I buy?" and start asking, "What is this system trying to express?" I know we've finally begun. That's where real education starts, and where real adoption has a chance to become durable.
