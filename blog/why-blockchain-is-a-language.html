Essay №001 Blockchain · Education 9 min read

Why blockchain is a language, not a product.

For fifteen years we've tried to explain public blockchains as if they were companies, products, or rails. The mental model that actually survives contact with reality is older and more useful: treat them as a language.

By David Justin 14 · October · 2025 Kuala Lumpur

The first blockchain lesson I ever taught was a disaster. I stood in front of twenty-two people in a Bangsar co-working space and spent forty minutes explaining hash functions, Merkle trees, and proof-of-work. By the end, three people had left. The rest were polite. Nobody had understood a thing.

That evening I walked home along Jalan Telawi and did what most educators never do: I asked myself, honestly, whether the fault was mine or theirs. The answer, of course, was neither. The fault was in the frame. I had tried to explain a language by listing its grammar rules before letting anyone hear it spoken.

A different first sentence

When you teach Mandarin to a beginner, you don't open with a diagram of tones. You say ni hao, let them repeat it, and watch their face change. The face change is the lesson. The grammar is the footnote.

Blockchain education has spent a decade opening with the grammar. We teach SHA-256 before we teach what "send" means. We teach consensus before we teach why you'd ever want two strangers to agree on anything. We do this because we are proud of the grammar — it is genuinely beautiful — and because explaining something complicated in simple terms can feel like a betrayal of the complexity.

It is the opposite of a betrayal. It is the only way anyone ever learned anything.

A product is something you buy. A language is something you think in. Treating public blockchains as the first is how you end up with speculation. Treating them as the second is how you end up with adoption.

What a language actually is

Every working language has four properties that the earliest linguists — Saussure, Jakobson, later Chomsky — eventually agreed on. It is shared (more than one person has to use it for it to mean anything). It is rule-governed (arbitrary, but consistent). It is generative (finite rules, infinite outputs). And it is public (nobody owns English; anybody who learns it inherits it).

Re-read that paragraph with the word "blockchain" substituted for "language" and tell me it isn't a better definition than anything you've read in a white paper. A public blockchain is shared (nodes), rule-governed (protocol), generative (any smart contract you can imagine), and public (forkable, readable, joinable by anyone who bothers to learn).

The reason so many people still think of blockchain as a product is that the industry itself sells it that way. Exchanges sell tokens. Foundations sell ecosystems. Venture capital sells equity in the infrastructure. Nobody sells the thing itself, because the thing itself — the language — is free.

The thing itself is free. That's the whole point. That's also why it's so hard to teach.

Three practical consequences

If you accept the linguistic frame, three things about how we educate, invest, and build change immediately. None of them are abstract.

One · Fluency beats literacy.

Literacy is knowing what words mean. Fluency is knowing what to say next. The blockchain industry is drowning in literate people — they can define NFT, DAO, TVL, MEV — and starving for fluent ones. A fluent person can look at a proposal and instantly sense whether it respects the grammar of the system or violates it. That instinct cannot be taught by vocabulary cards. It is taught by immersion, and immersion is expensive only because we refuse to build the immersion programmes.

Two · The best curriculum is a conversation.

The reason I eventually stopped opening my lessons with hash functions is that I noticed the learners who retained the most were the ones who had been spoken to, not lectured at. Blockchain is a language, which means the classroom where it is best taught looks nothing like a classroom and everything like a dinner table. At our academy we now structure every cohort around three weekly conversations. The slides are a reference, not a script.

Three · Countries that teach it first will compound.

English gave Britain and then America a hundred-year head start on global commerce. Mandarin is doing the same for regional influence right now. Whichever jurisdictions teach the grammar of public blockchains to their population first will have a similar compounding advantage for the next economic cycle — not because blockchain replaces money, but because it reshapes the way money moves. Malaysia is well-placed here and has been under-investing in it. That's a policy argument for another essay.

The hardest objection

The strongest pushback I get to this frame comes from builders, not skeptics. They tell me: "David, a language doesn't have market cycles. A language doesn't rug you. A language doesn't have a ticker. Your frame is elegant and wrong."

They're half right. The frame is incomplete. Blockchains are languages with assets attached — which is why speculation is not an aberration but a permanent feature. Every spoken language eventually attaches to money (publishing, translation, advertising); public blockchains just do it faster and more transparently. The speculation isn't the language. It's the publishing industry that grew up around the language.

Once you separate those two layers, everything becomes easier to reason about. You can love the language and hate the publishing industry. You can teach the grammar and warn about the markets. You can be fluent without being long.

What this means for our work

At the academy we've been quietly rebuilding our curriculum around this frame for the last eighteen months. The changes are small but the results have been dramatic. We opened cohort thirteen with a single sentence about "sending value the way you send a message" and watched retention climb by a third. We retired the word tokenomics from beginner classes. We added a weekly reading circle on the history of money, the history of the internet, and the history of writing itself — because the clearest way to understand a new language is to watch how old ones were born.

If you're teaching, building, or investing in this space, I'd invite you to try the same substitution. Next time you're about to say the word "blockchain" in a sentence, swap in "language" and see if the sentence still makes sense. Most of the time it will, and the ones where it doesn't are exactly the sentences worth re-examining.

That's the whole essay. Thanks for reading.
